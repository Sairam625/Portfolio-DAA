Inventory Management System for a Warehouse

The Inventory Management System is designed to align with SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production by ensuring minimal waste, efficient use of resources, and automated replenishment of stock. The system uses several algorithms to optimize the operations of the warehouse.

Key Features

  • Minimizing Waste: Products are used before their expiry dates using Insertion Sort.
  • Efficient Use of Resources: Warehouse layout optimization using Kruskal's Algorithm ensures efficient use of space and faster retrieval times.
  • Efficient Replenishment: Decrease and Conquer strategy dynamically manages stock levels.

Challenges

  • Reducing waste through expiry date management and ensuring perishable goods are used in time.
  • Optimizing warehouse space and workflows using Kruskal's Algorithm for efficient product retrieval.
  • Automating inventory replenishment to maintain optimal stock levels.
  • Tracking real-time data for accurate stock management, minimizing the risk of stockouts or overstocking.

Algorithms Used

  1. Decrease and Conquer (Inventory Replenishment Strategy): Tracks stock levels and automatically replenishes inventory as it gets low.
  2. Partitioning (Categorization of Inventory): Categorizes inventory into different product types for better management and tracking.
  3. Red-Black Tree (Efficient Search and Update): Stores and searches product details efficiently, allowing for quick updates when products are added or removed.
  4. Insertion Sort (Sorting Products by Expiry Dates): Sorts products by expiry dates to ensure perishable items are used first, reducing waste.
  5. Kruskal’s Algorithm (Optimizing Warehouse Layout): Optimizes warehouse layout to find the most efficient paths between product categories, improving warehouse operations.

Efficiency of Each Algorithm

1. Decrease and Conquer (Inventory Replenishment)

Time Complexity: O(n), where n is the number of products.

Space Complexity: O(1) for the function itself, since it doesn't require extra space other than the product data.

2. Insertion Sort (Sorting Products by Expiry Date)

Time Complexity: O(n²), where n is the number of products.

Space Complexity: O(1), as it is an in-place sorting algorithm.

3. Categorization (Partitioning Inventory by Type)

Time Complexity: O(n), where n is the number of products.

Space Complexity: O(n) due to the space required for the map to hold all the categorized products.

4. Kruskal's Algorithm (Optimizing Warehouse Layout)

Time Complexity: O(E log E + V), where E is the number of edges and V is the number of vertices (nodes).

Space Complexity: O(V + E), where V is the number of nodes and E is the number of edges. This space is required for storing the edges and the parent map for the union-find structure.
