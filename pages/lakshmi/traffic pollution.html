Restriction of Vehicles to Maintain Pollution

Business Case: Restricting Vehicles to Avoid Pollution

SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities) focuses on making cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient, and sustainable. Reducing vehicle emissions and optimizing traffic are key contributors to cleaner air and reduced pollution, which aligns with this goal.

Business Case Solution Using Algorithms

  • Queue and Stack (Traffic Control Systems):

    Queue: Use a queue data structure to model vehicles waiting at traffic signals. By managing the flow of traffic efficiently, you can minimize congestion and pollution.

    Stack: Implementing a stack for traffic signals or restrictions can help manage alternate-day vehicle access, i.e., even-numbered vehicles on one day, odd-numbered on another.

  • Dijkstra’s Algorithm (Shortest Path for Restricted Vehicles):

    Use Dijkstra's algorithm to find the shortest path for restricted vehicles (or alternatively, paths with less pollution) while taking into account vehicle restrictions and road capacities.

  • Heap (Prioritizing Vehicles):

    Use Heap to prioritize vehicles that have higher emissions or to determine which vehicles should be restricted based on their pollution levels.

  • Binary Search Tree (Vehicle Tracking):

    Implement a Binary Search Tree (BST) to efficiently track and manage vehicle registration numbers based on pollution levels. BST can provide a quick lookup of restricted vehicles.

  • AVL Tree (Dynamic Adjustments):

    Use an AVL Tree for maintaining a balanced and dynamic record of vehicles in real-time, especially when traffic laws or restrictions change periodically.

Solution Overview

By applying the above algorithms, cities can effectively manage traffic, reduce vehicle emissions, and align with SDG 11. The approach includes using efficient data structures for managing vehicle data and traffic flow.

Efficiency of Algorithms Used

  • Queue and Stack:

    Time Efficiency: \( O(1) \) for insertion and removal operations, suitable for dynamic traffic management.

    Space Efficiency: \( O(n) \), where \( n \) is the number of vehicles waiting or restricted.

  • Dijkstra’s Algorithm:

    Time Efficiency: Optimized to \( O(E + V \log V) \) using a priority queue.

    Space Efficiency: \( O(V + E) \), where \( V \) is vertices and \( E \) is edges.

  • Heap:

    Time Efficiency: \( O(\log n) \) for insertion and deletion.

    Space Efficiency: \( O(n) \), where \( n \) is the number of elements in the heap.

  • Binary Search Tree (BST):

    Time Efficiency: \( O(\log n) \) average; \( O(n) \) worst case.

    Space Efficiency: \( O(n) \) for storing nodes.

  • AVL Tree:

    Time Efficiency: Guarantees \( O(\log n) \) for insertion, deletion, and search.

    Space Efficiency: \( O(n) \) with a small constant overhead for balance factors.
